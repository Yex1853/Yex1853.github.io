Black Box Investigation

The Mystery Black Box, built by Mr. Sinusas, is a box filled with structures that we don't know. Our goal was to try and make a guess on what is in the black box. I investigated the box by pouring water into the box and seeing how much water comes out in order to brainstorm possible structures and I eventually made a replica to test if I was right.

Claim

One key structure I thought was in the black box was a small container on a wooden axle. Its function is to move water into the output funnel (The exit) and to store water. This could be in the black box.

Evidence

During my observations, I tested the box the way I mentioned before, pouring water in and investigating the amount that comes out. Though, I saw a weird phenomenon after we put the data into a spreadsheet. The water that comes out of the black box is sometimes more than what was poured in. My approach to figuring out how the black box works is looking into this phenomenon while brainstorming a design. I designed and built my model by first making a design on paper and on Photoshop, an Adobe program. Then I used these designs as inspiration and any materials that I thought would be necessary to build the model. My models' key structures were an input funnel on the top, a small container inside placed onto a wooden axle, and an output funnel. The input funnel to transport water into the box, the small container to transport the water out and to hold water, the axle to let the container rotate at around 145 degrees of rotation at a pivot, and an output funnel as an exit point. I tested the replica black box the same way as the original, pouring water inside and seeing how much comes out. The results were very interesting, as the data showed some kind of storage in the box. For example, in trial 6 onward, the input of water was 100 ml and the output was the same, but the water stored was 500 ml, which was a lot higher than what we put in. This wasn't exactly how the original black box stored the water however, as the original was able to let this water out. Also, the data had NO variety somehow after trial 6, as every value was the same (Input was 100 ml, output was 100 ml, water stored was 500 ml for all of them).

Steps Summary

  • I tested the original black box
  • I made a draft of a design
  • I made a final design in Photoshop
  • I used the design and made a replica model
  • I tested the replica just like the original
  • Finally, I compared the two results to see if they were similar

Reasoning (Part 1 of 3)

Models are physical representations of a design. They are helpful because they can be used for data which has various uses, like showing variation (Used in the Design Process) in the data, but it also shows, when the model is a replica of another that has its structures hidden, like what we did, how close the replica is to the original model. Their strengths are being used to run tests, which gives a lot more evidence than, say, a design. But their limitations are that they are usually simpler than what they represent regarding data and structures because of oversimplification.

Reasoning (Part 2 of 3)

With my data, I can say that a structure close to my small container and wooden axle inside the replica is inside the box. This is because my model was able to replicate the "extra stored water" but some extra structures are needed to release it and to make the variety seen in the original when we tested it.

Reasoning (Part 3 of 3)

An alternative claim is that a tube that goes through both funnels and a few cups that store water is in the black box. If this claim was true, I would’ve observed from my tests that the water output would not have the same variety as the original, which I’ve already seen. This other claim might've been better at representing the black box, but I think my replica’s structures could still be in the box as the structures in this alternative design have a lot of similarities as mine. This means for my actual claim that it was a little too simple for it to be correct, but still had the structures that might be inside the original black box.

Digital Photography, Design, and Animation Work

2D Design in Photoshop

When we were first given this task of brainstorming the structures of the black box, I did some initial brainstorming for a design, and this section will go a little more in depth for that process and what we made after building and testing.

After making a design of what I thought was inside the black box I was introduced to Photoshop. It's normally used to edit images but I was able to use a brush tool to draw out my design. In unison with the brush tool I also used a drawing tablet which felt a lot more natural than a mouse and using that I made a basic design. I also used other tools like the Lasso Select Tool, specifically the Polygonal Lasso Tool to allow us to manipulate sections of an image by selecting parts of it, the eraser which removes any mistakes I've made with the brush, the Magic Wand which works like a select tool but filters by colors, the Move Tool to allow me to move any aspect of the drawing, the Text tool which allows me to type text and place it anywhere using the Move Tool, Layers so I can separate parts of my drawing, Frames which use layers to animate my drawing, Generative AI which can generate any image prompted, and the Puppet Warp which allows me to move an image on points and allow me to bring this design to "life".

Photography

Eventually I took this final design and made it into a full, real life cardboard model. During the making, I was tasked with using a Professional dSLR Camera to take photos of the building/testing process. When taking pictures I had to obey a few rules. I first had to keep in mind the rule of thirds. Imagine a 3 by 3 2D grid on the camera screen. i had to put our main subject/action along 1 or more intersections of the lines, and preferably the opposite third where the subject is looking. This convention could be sometimes broken however if necessary. I also had to keep angles in mind, the best one for the photo we're taking. We had to use at least two angles, and the types are the eye level angle, where the camera is placed where your eyes normally are, usually used for connection/identification of a subject, the low angle where the camera is placed lower than the eye level, which is used more for power/impressiveness of a subject, and high angles where the camera is placed above our eye level, which is normally used for vulnerability/openness of a subject. Lastly, I had to keep framing in mind, which is how close/far a subject is. Wide/long for the setting/the area, medium for who (the subject) and close to show what's happening, what something is, or showing emotion.

3D Design with Maya

After making, testing and taking data of my model, I moved to a new software called Maya. It is a 3D software that has similarities to Blender. In the software, I made a new 3D design of my model, using a lot more tools than in Photoshop. I added shapes, for example, cubes , spheres and cones, manipulated them with the move tool, the rotate tool, and the scale tool, modifying these objects using the attribute editor and channel box which had a bunch of other dimensions, and materials which allowed me to change the color of an object. It also had extruding which allowed me to “deform” an object. I used these tools to make basic parts of my model like the cardboard box, funnels, and wooden axle. Then we moved on to more complicated manipulations, like bevelling, which changes the edges of an object to a "sloped" one, multicut which cuts, slices, and inserts edge loops, connect which, well, connects surfaces by inserting an edge between them, working as a bridge, curve/sweep meshes which make curved objects, and booleans which can use two objects and makes a hole using the difference. I used these tools for other aspects of my model, like an output tube for the output funnel, a small container that rotates on the wooden axle, and a graduated cylinder which poured water.

For materials, they have many properties like an object which can be edited, but there are a lot that I didn't use. The one I did use however was roughness, which told the material how shiny the object was meant to be. I had to make the material for the cardboard less shiny since cardboard usually doesn’t have shine in real life. In unison with materials we also used lighting, specifically skybox lighting to light up the entire scene, and directional lighting to light what was inside the box. This was used for rendering, which for that we switched the rendering from viewport to Arnold.

Like Photoshop I used animation, but it functioned a lot more differently. Every single time I move an object we can use keyframes which work as the layers in Photoshop. It was a way easier system. We also rigged a hand using bones and skin bind to allow for a hand with moving fingers. This hand represented me pouring in the water into the box and I was able to control the fingers in a way to show the hand was holding a graduated cylinder. I also animated every other aspect of the box to show its function and animated a camera (using rules stated before) + an arrow to show important subjects.

I attempted to use the Bifrost liquid simulation, which has “cells” that had many functions. There were preset cells meant for water creation, input/output cells, and I was able to drag and drop objects to connect to geometry for the water or to make collisions.
